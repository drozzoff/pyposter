FLAT C/HE SPILLS FOR ONLINE RANGE MONITORING IN PARTICLE THERAPY
A. Pastushenko, C. Graeff, B. Gålnander, R. Hetzel, D. Ondreka, J. Stadlmann, L. Volz
GSI Helmholtz Centre for Heavy Ion Research, Darmstadt, Germany
E. Renner
TU Wien, Vienna, Austria
ABSTRACT
The use of mixed ion beams is a developing approach aimed at improving current carbon-ion therapy by enabling online monitoring of dose deposition. In such a configuration, the main dose is still delivered by carbon ions, while lighter ions traverse the patient with negligible dose contribution and can be used for imaging. The main challenges of this approach include the need to simultaneously accelerate both ion species and to slowly extract them with a stable species ratio, a prerequisite for potential clinical application. At GSI, a mixed beam of 12C3+ and 4He+ ions was provided in May 2025 for biophysics experiments. The very small mass-to-charge difference of 0.065 % between these ion species enables simultaneous acceleration in the SIS18 heavy-ion synchrotron. To slowly extract them with transverse RF-knockout, chromaticity in the ring was adjusted, and the spill optimization system was used. These measures enabled a nearly rectangular spill with a constant helium/carbon ratio within the spill, confirmed by ionization-chamber measurements in the medical cave. This contribution presents the experimental results and discusses their implications for mixed-beam operation.
Mixed beam use for particle therapy

▲ Carbon-ion therapy enables highly localized dose deposition, but range uncertainties may shift the Bragg peak outside the target volume.
▲ One proposed solution is a mixed carbon–helium beam: carbon delivers the therapeutic dose, while helium is used for range monitoring.
▲ Potential clinical application requires simultaneous acceleration and slow extraction with a stable ion-species ratio.
▲ At GSI, the mixed beam is composed of 12C3+ and 4He+. Their mass-to-charge ratios differ by only 0.065%, making the two species nearly indistinguishable from the machine point of view.
▲ A dedicated setup in Cave M was used to study the extracted beam.

Tests at GSI in 2023 proved acceleration and extraction is possible [1]. So in May 2025 we attempted to fine-tune the extraction to achieve a stable helium-to-carbon ratio.


Machine properties

▲ Mixed C/He beam injected from UNILAC at 11.5 MeV/u and accelerated up to 225 and 260 MeV/u in SIS18 for extraction towards Cave M.
▲ Slow extraction was performed using RF knockout, giving a 2 s spill. RBPSK was used during the chromaticity scan, SOS [2] for the final optimized spill.

▲ Helium behaves similarly to off-momentum carbon, making the mixed-beam extraction highly sensitive to chromaticity.
▲ He stable area near 3rd-integer resonance shrinks with chromaticity.

Experimental setup

▲ Ionization chambers: IC1 - nozzle, IC2 - carbon Bragg peak, IC3 - helium Bragg peak.
▲ IC2 signal is dominated by carbon signal, IC3 is a mix of signals from helium and light fragments.


Chromaticity measurements

▲ Before optimizing the He/C ratio, chromaticity was measured to verify that the actual machine setting matched the model.

▲ A systematic offset of about 0.5 was observed between measured and modeled chromaticity.
▲ The mismatch is consistent with the presence of the first allowed sextupolar harmonic in the dipoles.

Chromaticity scan of the He/C spill ratio

▲ Chromaticity was scanned over the range ±2, with 15–20 cycles recorded for each setting.
▲ The He/C ratio was reconstructed from normalized IC signals.

▲ Helium is extracted later for negative chromaticity and earlier for positive chromaticity.
▲ The operational optimum was found at ξx ≈ 0.6, where the He/C ratio remains approximately constant within the spill.
▲ In contrast to simulations, the optimum was found at slightly overcompensated chromaticity, possibly due to differences in phase-space occupancy or nonlinear effects.

Final optimized spill

▲ After chromaticity optimization, SOS was switched on and used instead of RBPSK.

▲ SOS reduced intensity fluctuations in the extracted beam and produced a nearly rectangular carbon spill.
▲ The He/C ratio remained stable during the final optimized spill.

Conclusions

▲ Stable slow extraction of a mixed C/He beam was demonstrated at SIS18.
▲ The He/C spill ratio was optimized by chromaticity tuning.
▲ The operational optimum was found at ξx ≈ 0.6.
▲ SOS produced a nearly rectangular carbon spill while preserving a stable He/C ratio.

These results support future mixed-beam studies for ion-therapy applications.
